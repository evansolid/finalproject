How is Your Privacy at Risk and What Happens if it is Released?


Data breaches in healthcare have been getting more and more frequent. Between October 2009 and January 2026, there have been over 7000 large healthcare data breaches, and these are just the ones that were reported. One of the biggest healthcare breaches to date occurred in 2024, when Change Healthcare (part of the UnitedHealth Group) was subject to a healthcare breach. Change handled approximately 1 in 3 patient records, which range from insurance claims to prescriptions and so on. A Russian ransomware group encrypted and incapacitated much of Change’s data, which had massive impacts on healthcare providers and patients across the country. Many were subject to delayed authorization of necessary care, months-long delays of day-to-day operations, and revenue loss and other financial impacts. It’s estimated that 192.7 million people were affected by the incident.

Data from OCR suggests that data breaches are moving away from theft and towards hacking or IT incidents, and that in recent years data breaches have started affecting more people on average than 20 years ago. These violations can also cost healthcare providers millions of dollars penalty fees for violating HIPAA. With the rapid growth of AI and a shortage of healthcare workers, it’s difficult for many providers to keep up to date with accurate security measures. Additionally, the desire to keep up with current technology can incentivize these companies to merely check the boxes when it comes to meeting HIPAA compliance without actually ensuring that patient records are secure.

The actual consequences of private health records being stolen or made public can vary, but security breaches often end up putting patients at risk of identity theft. According to the National Association of Healthcare Access Management, personal medical data is considered much more valuable to hackers than credit card information, and the consequences of stolen medical information are a lot more difficult to reverse. Most people don’t realize when their medical records have been stolen, and it’s a lot harder to stop this information from being used since medical records can’t be cancelled in the way a credit card can. Since medical records typically include social security numbers, dates of birth, addresses, medical conditions, and phone numbers, there are a lot of ways this data can be used by hackers or people who can otherwise get their hands on patient records. For example, stolen medical information can be used to make fake insurance claims, which often leaves victims without coverage when they need it. This data can also be used to get and sell prescription drugs or can be sold to businesses to help market to consumers. With all of the risks attached to private health record leaks, it’s important that we put safeguards in place to protect patients as much as possible.
