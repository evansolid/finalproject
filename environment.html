How the Environment is Affected


It’s not far from common knowledge that AI can be hugely damaging to the environment, but many people don’t know the specifics. Data centers used to house and support AI take massive amounts of energy; the Organization for Economic Co-Operation and Development projected that data centers would be the fifth largest electricity consumer in the world, between Japan and Russia. In addition, the training of GPT-3, an OpenAI chatbot, took 1287 megawatt hours of electricity, which is enough to power about 120 average US homes for a year. It also produced over 550 tons of carbon dioxide. Even once the models are trained, they still take a lot of energy. One query on an AI chatbot can take up to five times the energy required by a Google search. Additionally, with the rapid rise and evolution of AI, new models are being developed and trained in quick succession, which renders all of the energy and resources used on training the previous models a waste. Since water is used to cool computing equipment, that’s another factor that plays into the environmental impact of AI. Noman Bashir, the lead author of a 2024 paper about the sustainability implications of AI, estimates that every kilowatt-hour of energy used by a data center requires two liters of water to cool it.
