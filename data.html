How AI (and Your Data) is Being Used in Healthcare


AI can be used to assist people in diagnosing and receiving treatment. An example of a helpful use of AI in health services is period tracking apps, which use artificial intelligence models to track and predict their cycles. This can be very useful for people who menstruate because it allows them to gain more knowledge on their unique cycle and share that information with healthcare service providers. Unfortunately, these apps have been criticized for using biased algorithms that may reinforce structural inequality among people of color, immigrants, or other vulnerable groups. In addition, several period tracking technologies and apps have come under fire for selling user data to marketing and advertising companies. They do not have transparent privacy policies and many people were unaware that their private information was being collected and shared in this way. Other examples of the potential benefits of using AI are 3D-printing patient-specific devices for care or creating at-home testing kits for pregnancy, viruses, HIV, or other important information. These technologies would greatly benefit the general public when attempting to access care. The difficulty with developing and establishing this technology is that the FDA is very strict about approving drugs for use on patients and if a new treatment is developed, it can take several years to be approved. Many AI vendors claim that AI assistance would greatly speed up the process of developing, testing, approving, and deploying new treatments.

In order for AI technology to properly function, they need to train on large quantities of data. In the case of healthcare services, this data often takes the form of personal data, health data, and PHI, or Protected Health Information. PHI is protected under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. This act is often referred to as HIPAA, and is responsible for keeping confidential patient information private and out of the hands of malicious actors. In general, businesses deploying AI technology do not have the proper clearance under HIPAA to access the necessary information for training their models. Although providing them with this information would allow them to better utilize the technology, it can be difficult to know what a company does with the information it collects and there have been several examples of personal data being sold to advertising companies or AI companies with weak security being hacked to leak private information to the public.
